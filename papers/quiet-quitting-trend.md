# Summary

This paper investigates the drivers of "quiet quitting" — working strictly within assigned tasks with no extra effort or emotional investment — among millennial and Gen Z workers in Indonesia. Using self-determination theory as a lens and SEM on 600 survey responses, the authors test four hypotheses linking empowering leadership, organizational culture, and work overload to job satisfaction, and job satisfaction to quiet quitting. All four hypotheses are supported: higher job satisfaction reduces quiet quitting, empowering leadership and good organizational culture increase satisfaction, and work overload decreases it.

# Key Insights

- **Job satisfaction is a direct buffer against quiet quitting**: the more satisfied employees are, the less they disengage and coast — dissatisfied younger workers specifically lose motivation and reduce organizational participation.
- **Empowering leadership is the strongest positive driver**: autonomy, active participation in decision-making, open communication, and development support all raise satisfaction in both millennials and Gen Z, though with slightly different emphases (millennials want guidance + recognition, Gen Z want skill growth + inclusion).
- **Work overload is a direct threat to satisfaction**: time pressure, physical and mental exhaustion, and loss of work-life balance push both generations toward quiet quitting — the "lying flat" phase where career ambition drops entirely.
- **Organizational culture matters for value alignment**: millennials and Gen Z want their personal values (diversity, flexibility, collaboration, tech-forward progression) to match the organization's culture. Misalignment causes discomfort and dissatisfaction even when compensation is adequate.
- **Quiet quitting is framed as a symptom, not a cause**: it's the behavioral outcome of unmet psychological needs (autonomy, competence, relatedness) — rooted in self-determination theory — rather than laziness or poor character.

# Methodology

- **Design**: Quantitative, cross-sectional survey study.
- **Sample**: 600 respondents, purposive sampling; age 17–42; minimum 1 year tenure; predominantly Java (49%) and Sumatra (28%); data collected May–September 2023.
- **Scale**: 10-point semantic differential scale across 56 questionnaire items covering five constructs: quiet quitting (13 items), empowering leadership (18 items), work overload (4 items), organizational culture (12 items), job satisfaction (9 items).
- **Analysis**: Covariance-based SEM using AMOS 24. Full pipeline: normality testing → outlier detection → validity (factor loadings > 0.4, AVE > 0.4) → reliability (CR > 0.70) → goodness-of-fit evaluation → path analysis.
- **Model fit**: RMSEA = 0.073 (fit); other indices (GFI, CFI, TLI) were marginal, which the authors acknowledge.
- **Bias mitigation**: Reverse-scored items and clear separation of dependent/independent variables in the questionnaire.

# Results

| Hypothesis | Relationship | CR | p-value | Decision |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| H1 | Job Satisfaction → Quiet Quitting | −2.219 | 0.026* | Accepted |
| H2 | Empowering Leadership → Job Satisfaction | 4.554 | <0.000*** | Accepted |
| H3 | Work Overload → Job Satisfaction | −3.318 | <0.000*** | Accepted |
| H4 | Organizational Culture → Job Satisfaction | 5.468 | <0.000*** | Accepted |

All four hypotheses supported. Organizational culture had the largest CR magnitude on job satisfaction, followed by empowering leadership, and then work overload.

# Strengths

- Large, geographically diverse Indonesian sample (600 respondents, all major island regions represented) — gives the findings regional validity rare in quiet quitting studies.
- Rigorous SEM pipeline with clear reporting of assumption tests, factor loadings, CR, and AVE.
- Self-determination theory grounding gives the conceptual model solid theoretical footing and connects the variables coherently.
- Practical: findings directly translate into HR strategies (workload management, leadership training, culture audits).
- Quiet quitting measured via four validated behavioral indicators (working hours compliance, task adherence, loss of ambition, disengagement) rather than a single self-report item.

# Limitations

- **Marginal model fit**: GFI (0.727), CFI (0.732), and TLI (0.732) are all below the recommended 0.90+ threshold — the structural model fit is weak.
- **Cross-sectional design**: causality cannot be established; snapshot data can't track how quiet quitting evolves over time.
- **Self-report bias**: all constructs measured through self-administered questionnaires; social desirability may inflate job satisfaction and underreport disengagement.
- **No salary/compensation variable**: the paper acknowledges salary satisfaction matters to younger workers but doesn't model it as a standalone predictor.
- **Indonesia-specific**: sampling is heavily Java/Sumatra-skewed; findings may not generalize to other cultural contexts.
- **Generational boundary is blurry**: pooling millennials (26–42) and Gen Z (17–25) without fully disaggregating results by generation loses nuance the paper's own literature review suggests exists.

# Personal Notes

Useful framing for thinking about burnout-adjacent behaviors in tech workplaces — the "lying flat" descriptor maps well onto what happens in high-pressure internship or early-career roles where the cost-benefit of going above and beyond starts to feel negative. The self-determination theory lens (autonomy, competence, relatedness) is actually a cleaner framework than I expected for this topic.

Relevant to my own context: the finding that Gen Z specifically seeks continuous skill development and tech-forward organizational culture as satisfaction drivers lines up with why flat, process-heavy work environments feel demotivating even when pay is decent.

The marginal fit indices are a real weakness — would be worth looking for replication studies in other regions or with better-fitting models before citing the structural paths as robust.

Not directly applicable to NLP/AI research, but useful background if I ever need to write about human factors in AI adoption or workplace automation impact.
